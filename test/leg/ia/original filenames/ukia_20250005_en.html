Questions

1. What were the policy objectives of the measure? (Maximum 5 lines)

The 2013 Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Regulations came into force on the 1st of January 2014. The objectives of the Regulations were: • to ensure compliance with the recast WEEE Directive 2012/19/EU (‘the WEEE Directive’) which aimed to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of the original WEEE Directive by further protecting the environment as well as human and animal health; and • to respond to the concerns expressed by UK electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) producers under the Red Tape Challenge about the cost of meeting their financial obligations under the 2006 WEEE Regulations (the Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Regulations 2006 (SI 2006 No. 3289, as amended by SIs 2007 No. 3454, 2009 No. 2957, 2009 No. 3216 and 2010 No. 1155)).

2. What evidence has informed the PIR? (Maximum 5 lines)

The Consultation1 on Reforming the Producer Responsibility System for Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment 2023 and Electrical Waste: Reforming the Producer Responsibility System – Call for Evidence2, the accompanying Impact Assessment3 and Summary of Responses to the consultation4 and call for evidence5 provide the evidence. Those documents with the Government Response constitute the statutory requirements for a PIR under Regulation 93 of the 2013 WEEE Regulations. The consultation and call for evidence take forward policy recommendations from the previous PIR and other measures aimed to drive up levels of separately collected WEEE for re-use/recycling and wider measures designed to embrace the circular economy.

3. To what extent have the policy objectives been achieved? (Maximum 5 lines)

EU Member State collection targets (45%) were achieved by setting household WEEE collection targets from 2014 onwards and gathering data on household and non household WEEE financed by producers, supplemented by data from other sources. Along with most other member states, the UK was unable to achieve the higher target (65%) between 2019 and leaving the EU. The compliance fee and household WEEE collection targets have been instrumental in decreasing cost of evidence and enabling costs correction of WEEE evidence. There has been high compliance among UK based business but compliance by online businesses - particularly those overseas and more recently amongst producers of vapes - has been lower.

Questions

4. What were the original assumptions? (Maximum 5 lines)

The original assumptions were set out in two impact assessments accompanying the Government response to the implementation of the WEEE Directive and changes to the UK WEEE system. One was an Impact Assessment of the recast Directive 2012/96/EU on WEEE and the other an Impact Assessment of the UK WEEE System. A key assumption was that a compliance fee would be charged to individual producer compliance schemes (PCSs) in the event that they did not meet their household WEEE collection target. The compliance fee would be set at a level that put a ‘price ceiling’ on the cost of evidence in order to restrict the risk that costs of collection and treatment paid by producers through PCSs was often much higher that the true costs of collection and treatment of WEEE. Within the Impact Assessment which accompanied the Government response to the implementation of the WEEE Directive and changes to the UK WEEE system, the central scenario assumed an additional 10% of WEEE tonnage of gas discharge lamps (GDLs), displays and cooling products would be funded through the compliance fee. Photovoltaic (PV) panels come into scope with the introduction of the 2013 Regulations. It was assumed that 45% of PV EEE put on market (POM) would be collected as WEEE.

5. Were there any unintended consequences? (Maximum 5 lines)

The previous PIR highlighted the burdensome way that Regulation 34 worked for local authorities which has been addressed. This provides a guarantee that PCSs would always finance the costs of collection and subsequent treatment of WEEE arising at “Designated Collection Facilities” even in circumstances where no individual PCS was willing to enter a contact. Whilst measures have largely been successful in ensuring the system brings proportionate costs to businesses, the system does not provide levers that maximise the level of separately collected WEEE financed by producers or that ensure re-use is prioritised over recycling. Changes are necessary to ensure better communication with householders on how to dispose of WEEE and measures that make it easier for householders and business to send unwanted equipment for reuse/recycling. The focus on promoting recycling over landfill has resulted in a lack of measures within the legislation designed to support the circular economy.

6. Has the evidence identified any opportunities for reducing the burden on business? (Maximum 5 lines)

The significant rise in internet sales since the inception of the original regulations and the surge on the sale of vapes has recreated some unfair cost distribution (and consequential burdens) across those organisations defined as producers. The consultation proposals and subsequent government response seek to address those anomalies by creating a new category of equipment for vapes and creating a new category of producer for online market places who would in future have a financial obligation for WEEE collections in respect of the equipment placed on their UK market by overseas sellers. Wider potential opportunities have been identified to reduce compliance costs placed on businesses that adopt circular economy business models and those supplying equipment that meet specific eco-design requirements.

7. How does the UK approach compare with the implementation of similar measures internationally, including how EU member states implemented EU requirements that are comparable or now form part of retained EU law, or how other countries have implemented international agreements? (Maximum 5 lines)

The regulations fully implemented the WEEE Directive. The Directive aims to reduce the negative impacts on the environment as well as human and animal health (negative externalities) caused by the disposal of WEEE and it provides significant flexibility as to how it is implemented by Member States which has resulted in different methods for implementing the requirements. There is no evidence available to provide accurate cost comparisons between Member States. Further information on how other Member States have implemented the WEEE Directive are set out in the ‘Comparisons with other Member States’ section of the 2020 PIR. Research commissioned by Defra6 on “ingredients for success” in reforming the 2013 WEEE Regulations sets out some comparisons with EU Member States. In the absence of changes to the EU Directive, there will not be substantial changes to member state approaches to implementation since then.
