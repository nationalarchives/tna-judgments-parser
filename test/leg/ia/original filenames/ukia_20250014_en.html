Equality Impact Assessment

Contents

Summary of policy changes and equality impacts

Under the Equality Act 2010, the Department for Education (DfE), as a public authority, is legally obliged to give due regard to equality issues when making policy decisions. This is known as the Public Sector Equality Duty (PSED).

This equality impact assessment (EIA) covers the following planned changes to tuition fees and student finance arrangements in England for new and continuing students in academic year 2025/26:

•

Increasing maximum tuition fee limits for full-time, full-time accelerated and part-time undergraduate courses by forecast inflation (3.1%)

•

Increasing maximum tuition fee loan limits for full-time, full-time accelerated and part-time undergraduate courses by forecast inflation (3.1%)

•

Lowering maximum tuition fee limits for foundation years in classroom-based subjects with maximum tuition fee limits for foundation years in all other subjects increasing by forecast inflation (3.1%)

•

Increasing maximum loans for living costs for full-time and part-time students on undergraduate courses and loans for students on postgraduate master’s and doctoral degree courses by forecast inflation (3.1%)

•

Increasing targeted grants for full-time undergraduate students with adult and child dependants and disabled students’ allowance for full-time and part-time undergraduate and postgraduate students by forecast inflation (3.1%)

Overall assessment

The increase in maximum tuition fees and loan limits for full-time, full-time accelerated and part-time undergraduate courses by forecast RPIX inflation of 3.1%1 is expected to have a broadly neutral impact as the increase is in nominal terms meaning the total level of debt for Student Loan Company (SLC) funded students should remain unchanged in real terms.

We do not believe that the increase in tuition fee limits will significantly alter participation decisions for most students as the value of tuition fees will remain unchanged in real terms and the commensurate increase in tuition fee loans will ensure that higher education (HE) remains free at the point of access for those eligible for SLC support.

It is possible that SLC borrowers considered to be comparatively more debt averse – specifically students who are female, older, Black, from an ethnic minority or have a known disability – could be negatively or disproportionately negatively impacted if they perceive the cost of study to have increased to the point that they choose not to participate in HE because they now deem it unaffordable. However, we consider this risk to be low as the latest available data shows that HE acceptance numbers and participation rates are higher today than they were in 2010/11 before tuition fees rose to £9,000.

The lowering of tuition fees for classroom-based foundation years is expected to lower in nominal and real terms the total level of debt accrued by SLC funded students who choose this entry route into HE. We expect those SLC borrowers choosing this pathway into HE who are comparatively more debt averse to be positively or disproportionately positively impacted by this planned change.

Prospective foundation years students who are comparatively less geographically mobile could be negatively or disproportionately negatively impacted if the lowering of tuition fees leads to a contraction in provision which limits access to HE in their area. We however deem the risk to be low as we believe there are a sufficient alternative entry routes into HE besides foundation years available at comparatively lower cost to students.

The increase in loans for living costs for undergraduate students, loans for postgraduate master’s and doctoral degree students, targeted grants and disabled students’ allowance by forecast RPIX inflation of 3.1% is expected to also have a broadly neutral impact. This is because the planned increase is in nominal terms meaning the level of debt for SLC borrowers should remain unchanged in real terms.

It is possible that comparatively more debt averse SLC borrowers could be negatively or disproportionately negatively impacted if they perceive the cost of study to have increased to the point that they are discouraged from pursuing HE because they now consider it too expensive. However, failing to increase loans for living costs by inflation would likely pose a greater risk, as the level of financial support provided would increasingly not meet students’ living costs.

In principle, the planned student finance changes should not be significant enough to alter participation decisions since the impact of increasing support by forecast inflation means a nominal but not a real terms increase in student debt. Moreover, the uplift to tuition fee and maintenance loans will only increase lifetime repayments for those borrowers who are already forecast to repay their loans in full. Lower lifetime earners will see no increase in their lifetime repayments due to the loan increases, and no borrowers will see higher monthly repayments when they start repaying their loans. 

While the increase in loan support should maintain the spending power of students at their current levels it will not offset the significant erosion in the real value of SLC support which has occurred in the last few years due to the spike in inflation. This means there is still a potentially higher likelihood that SLC borrowers who are comparatively more debt averse may be being discouraged from participating in HE because they do not view it as affordable.

So far, no clear evidence has yet emerged to indicate that this risk has materialised for students with protected characteristics or from disadvantaged groups. Data shows that HE acceptance numbers for the latest application cycle are higher than the previous year while progression rates into HE have held up for most protected groups.

The latest UCAS entry rate data for 2024 shows the entry rate for disadvantaged students who were previously in receipt of free school meals (FSM) and those from low HE participation areas increased compared to 2023 and are above pre-pandemic levels, although the gap between advantaged and disadvantaged students remains persistently large and stubborn.

Further research is however needed to understand whether the rising cost of study and student finance changes have started to influence students’ decision-making behaviour, the choices they make and the outcomes they achieve.

It is possible that students have started to make different decisions about HE but that it is too early or not possible to observe this in the data. One area of investigation would be to explore whether comparatively more debt averse students are now being more discouraged by the perceived increase in the cost of study which has had a dampening effect on HE participation rates.

Separately, survey evidence shows that students are increasingly turning to part-time employment to supplement the income they receive from their SLC loans, grants and allowances. However, we do not know the extent to which this is happening among students with protected characteristics or from disadvantaged groups.

Regarding the three limbs of the Public Sector Equality Duty, we do not consider that the planned changes will amount to unlawful discrimination. While certain groups are more likely to be affected, the planned changes are intended to safeguard the financial sustainability of the HE sector (which is in the best interests of all students) and ensure that cost and affordability are not barriers to participating in HE, especially for those who are comparatively more sensitive to the cost of study and debt averse.

We consider the student finance changes are required to continue advancing equality of opportunity. Given rising living costs due to inflation increases in loan and grant support are necessary to ensure that access to HE continues to be based on the ability to learn, not the ability to pay. In the absence of further increases in loan, grants and allowances HE may become less affordable, increasing the risk that some students, especially those groups who are deemed to be comparatively more debt averse, are discouraged from participating in HE, even though they would benefit from it.

We do not consider that the package of changes to tuition fees and student loan arrangements would have any significant impact on the need to foster good relations between persons who share a particular protected characteristic and those who do not. We do not consider that any frustrations felt by students who are more impacted positively or negatively by the planned changes would affect relationships with others who are less impacted by them.

There could be perceptions of unfairness if the policies are most likely to be seen as affecting certain students – for example those considering classroom-based foundation years as an entry route into HE. However, the aims of this policy are to reduce the disproportionate growth of foundation years that represent poor value for money, whilst ensuring that students who continue to access HE through this route pay a fairer price.

The influence of cost of study, debt and finance on higher education decision making

There is an extensive evidence base on the influence that the cost of study, debt aversion and the availability of student finance have on the decisions students make about HE participation. A summary of the key insights is set out below.

Overall attitudes to student debt

Attitudes to student loans, in general, have become more favourable over time. Research shows that individuals felt more comfortable taking on student loans to fund undergraduate studies in 2015 compared with 2002, despite the increase in costs over that period2. The large increase in tuition fees in 2012, offset by the increase in financial support, had no discernible negative impact on participation rates3. More recently, research shows student loan debt is often perceived as normal/ a ‘fact of life’4.

Impact of student debt on students with protected characteristics

Some individuals may be deterred from entering HE because of fear of debt. Individuals from disadvantaged backgrounds have historically been more debt averse but there is evidence that those from middle income backgrounds are also becoming concerned about student debt5. Despite these concerns, research tends to show that, for many individuals, the perceived benefits of HE outweigh the perceived costs6. However, those from disadvantaged backgrounds are more likely to report being deterred from planning to enter HE because of fear of debt7 and less likely to agree that taking up student loans to go to university is a good investment compared with those from more advantaged groups8. Mature learners are a group more debt-averse than younger learners and they tend to have more financial commitments9. Other groups shown in research to be less comfortable taking out student loans are non-white groups, female applicants and disabled applicants10.

The wider costs of HE

Some students feel constrained about HE choices due to affordability and/or costs. In 2022, around three in ten (30%) pupils and learners in years 12 and 13 in 2022 reported that their plans for education or training had changed because of the rising cost of living11. This was consistent across different groups (ethnicity, sex, whether receiving Free School Meals) of pupils and learners.

More recent research in 2024 showed that the cost of HE is one of the reasons why young people are not planning to apply to HE. Similarly, affordability was reported as a reason by around a fifth of students in year 13 who did not intend to apply to university in 202312. There is evidence to show that financial factors can impact on students’ choice of provider, location of study, and the subject studied, as well as intensity of study, i.e. whether to study full-time or part-time13,14.

Impact of costs on decision making for students with protected characteristics

Recent research shows that young people in the lowest income category were least likely to be planning to enter HE in 2023, as were those whose families use a food bank15. Disadvantaged individuals are more likely to constrain their HE choices compared with more advantaged students. Young people who plan to live at home whilst studying in HE are disproportionately more likely to be those from families facing financial challenges. Young people from families which reported using a food bank in the past year were much more likely to plan on living at home compared to those that did not use a food bank. The same was true for those from families which are behind with their housing payments16.

The case for uplifting tuition fees and student support

Tuition fee limits

The planned increase in tuition fee limits for full-time and part-time undergraduate courses and accelerated degree courses in England for students starting in academic year 2025/26 represents the first increase in domestic tuition fee caps since 2017. In the seven years since, the tuition fee cap of £9,250 has fallen in real-terms by an estimated 28%17.

The tuition fee increase is intended to improve the financial sustainability of HE providers at a time when the sector is facing increasing and wide-ranging financial challenges and cost pressures including: the impacts of inflation, higher staff pay and pension contributions, a challenging recruitment environment, increased financial support for students experiencing hardship, and rising operating and energy costs associated with maintaining and running their estate. According to the Office for Students, nearly three quarters of regulated HE providers in England could be in deficit by 2025-26 and many providers will need to take bold action to remain financial sustainable18.

Lowering tuition fee limits for foundation years in classroom-based subjects – such as business and administration, and social sciences – is intended to stem the disproportionate growth of foundation years in these subjects, and improve value for money to students and taxpayers by lowering the cost of study for this provision so that it is more in line with functionally similar alternatives such as Access to HE Diplomas which offer broadly the same outcomes.

Student loan and grant support

HE represents a significant investment in terms of time and money. On average, undergraduate HE borrowers who started their studies in academic year 2023/24 are forecast to enter repayment with an average debt of £43,700 (composed of loan outlay borrowed and interest accumulated during study19.

The planned increase in tuition fee loans will enable students eligible for SLC support to continue to fully cover the upfront cost of their chosen course, thereby ensuring that HE remains free at the point of access. The similar increase in loan support to cover living costs is intended to ensure that students can continue making the same spending decisions as they did previously with regards to accommodation, travel, food, and utilities – the costs of which will have been rising over time due to inflation20.

Analysis of equality impacts by proposed measure

Maximum Tuition fee and loan limits

In academic year 2022/23, nearly 993,000 full-time English domiciled undergraduate students had their tuition fees mainly funded by SLC22. This represented around 84.5% of the total English domiciled undergraduate students enrolled at an English provider.

New and existing students may face a nominal increase in tuition fees, depending on whether HE providers choose, or are able, to implement the fee uplift. For students who take out an SLC fee loan to cover the upfront cost of course fees, total student loan debt will also rise in nominal terms.

We do not expect the planned increase in tuition fees will be significant enough to alter participation decisions. First, the cost of study is one of many financial and non-financial factors which prospective students consider when making decisions about whether to participate in HE and is not necessarily the most influential one23.

Second, the increase in tuition fees will be accompanied by a commensurate increase in tuition fee loans ensuring that participation in HE remains free at the point of access for those eligible for SLC support. Finally, while tuition fees and tuition fee loans would rise in nominal terms they would remain unchanged in real terms as would the level of debt that students may take on to cover course costs.

There is little evidence to suggest that the previous increases in tuition fees and loans in 2012 and 2017 have had a negative impact on HE decision making. As Tables 3 and 4 below show, HE application numbers and participation rates for students with different characteristics are, in most cases, comparatively higher in 2022/23 than 2010/11 (before tuition fees were raised to £9,000).

It is possible that despite tuition fees, loans and student debt remaining unchanged in real terms, there is potentially a higher risk that some students may perceive the cost of study to have increased, to the extent that they may be more likely to be discouraged from pursuing HE because they now view it as too expensive, even though they would benefit from it.

This is because some groups, due to their personal characteristics or circumstances, are comparatively more likely to be cost sensitive and debt averse. Evidence shows that this is particularly likely to be the case for those groups which have work, family or financial commitments or would likely face comparatively higher costs of study.

Foundation years

All students choosing classroom-based foundation years as their entry route into HE will benefit from lower tuition fees and loans which should lower the total level of debt in nominal and real terms that SLC borrowers may take out should they progress to the first year of their preferred undergraduate course.

However, there is a possible risk that some HE providers could scale back or withdraw classroom-based foundation year provision in those cases where it is no longer deemed financially viable at the lower fee level, potentially reducing access and choice in some areas of the country. This could negatively impact some protected groups who, because of work, family and financial commitments, are less geographically mobile and unable to travel easily to where provision is still available.

However, the risk of this occurring is deemed to be low as we believe there are sufficient alternative entry routes into HE that are available, and at comparatively lower costs to students. These include, but are not limited to:

•

Direct entry in Year 1 – Some subject areas do not have subject-specific entry requirements. Therefore, we expect that some students who would otherwise have studied a classroom-based foundation year may be able to enter degree courses directly in year 1.

•

Access to HE Diplomas — AHEs are stand-alone Level 3 qualifications, delivered in a further education setting. Akin to foundation years, AHEs prepare students to progress to HE study. They are widely available for classroom-based subjects.

•

Higher Technical Qualifications (HTQs) and other Level 4 and 5 courses —HTQs and other Level 4/5 qualifications can promote entry into skilled employment or can be topped up to degree-level with further study, either on completion or later in life.

•

Foundation years in other subjects — We expect a wide range of classroom-based foundation year provision to be retained, at the lower £5,760 fee cap. Foundation years in all other subjects will be able to charge tuition fees up to the new maximum limit of £9,535 and students will receive fee loans up to this amount, so we do not expect the scale of this provision to significantly change.

•

A-Levels — The primary route of entry into HE. Prospective students who do not meet the entry requirements for their chosen degree course upon completion can choose to re-take their A Levels.

Student finance loans, grants and allowances

It is expected that the planned changes to student loans, grants and allowances for academic year 2025/26 will have a neutral impact.

In the same way as tuition fees and loans we do not expect the planned student finance changes to be significant enough to alter participation decisions for most students, including SLC borrowers. This is because the impact of increasing support by forecast inflation will mean a nominal but not a real terms increase in student debt.

No borrowers will see higher monthly repayments when they start repaying their loans due to the increases for 2025/26. This is because student loans are not like consumer loans; monthly repayments depend on borrowers’ earnings, not the amount borrowed or interest rates. Only those borrowers who are forecast to repay their loans in full – 65% of full-time undergraduates starting in 2023/2435 - will see an increase in their lifetime repayments due to the fee and loan increases for 2025/26. Borrowers who do not repay their loan in full will see any outstanding balance at the end of their loan term, including interest accrued, continue to be written off at no detriment to the individual.

While the increase in loan support will maintain the spending power of students at their current levels it will not offset the significant erosion in the real value of SLC support which has occurred in the last few years due to the spike in inflation. This means that there continues to be a higher risk that some students who are comparatively more debt averse could still perceive the cost of study to have increased to the extent that they choose not to participate in HE because they now deem it too expensive.

So far, no evidence has emerged to indicate that this risk has materialised. Data shows that the number of students receiving acceptances into HE and participation rates have remained broadly stable over the last few years across most student characteristics. One possible reason may be that students are increasingly turning to part-time employment to supplement their income from maintenance loans and grants. Survey evidence shows that 56% of respondents were in paid employment during term time in 2024 compared to 45% in 2022 while the average number hours worked per week have increased over the same period from 12.3 to 14.5%36. However, further research is needed to understand the extent to which this is happening among students with protected characteristics or from disadvantaged groups.

Family Test Analysis

Our Family Test Analysis indicates that in general a 3.1% increase to loans and grants for living costs for 2025/26 would not have a particular impact on families. The increase in debt resulting from a 3.1% increase in maximum loans would be in nominal terms only meaning that students with family commitments will be no worse or better off as a result.

Moreover, the income contingent nature of loan repayments means that the longer-term impact will only be felt by those students with family commitments who go on to earn more than the relevant repayment threshold for their loan type after they graduate. Repayment thresholds are set at £25,000 per year (repayable from April 2026) for undergraduate Plan 5 student loan borrowers starting new courses from the 2023/24 academic year onwards, £27,295 per year for undergraduate Plan 2 loans and £21,000 per year for postgraduate master’s degree and doctoral degree loans (Plan 3).

We don’t have evidence that the 3.1% uprating to loans and grants will have a differential impact on families, however students with dependants may be more vulnerable to financial risk. A 3.1% increase for 2025/26 to maximum dependants’ grants (ADG, CCG and PLA) and disabled students’ allowance would not have a significant enough impact on financial resources to influence family formation.
