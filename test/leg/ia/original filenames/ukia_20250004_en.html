What is the problem under consideration? Why is government intervention necessary?

• Safety and Security (S&S) declarations provide intelligence which is used by HMRC and Border Force to prevent illicit goods such as drugs and firearms entering and leaving the UK, and to tackle serious and organised crime and national security threats.

• In August 2023, the previous government published a new model for importing goods into GB, and also announced that Entry Summary (ENS) declarations (a type of S&S declaration for imports) would be required for EU imports into Great Britain (GB) from 31 October 2024.

• The current government delayed introducing ENS declarations on EU imports to 31 January 2025, to ensure businesses have enough time to prepare for the new requirements. Businesses have been encouraged to start submitting declarations as soon as they are ready.

What are the policy objectives and the intended effects?

• The new model for importing goods was designed to balance the need for effective border controls with the need to support businesses with import processes that are as simple as possible.

• The measures are intended to meet three objectives:

- Improve HMRC and Border Force’s ability to identify and stop high harm goods being brought into the country.

- Reduce the administrative burden for businesses required to submit ENS declarations and facilitate the flow of legitimate trade.

- Meet the UK’s international commitments to require S&S declarations on all imports entering GB and treat all trading partners equally.

What policy options have been considered, including any alternatives to regulation? Please justify preferred option (further details in Evidence Base)

• Two measures are being introduced:

- Ending the temporary waiver for ENS declarations on imports from the EU into Great Britain (GB) from 31 January 2025. The data from ENS declarations will provide critical intelligence to HMRC and Border Force to support better targeting of illicit goods. Maintaining the waiver (the ‘do nothing’ option) is not viable as it would not meet the UK’s international obligations and would limit HMRC and Border Force’s ability to risk-assess EU imports as they arrive in GB.

- Reducing the minimum dataset required to complete an ENS declaration on imports into GB. Businesses will have the option to submit additional data, which will improve HMRC and Border Force’s ability to risk the goods and reduce the chance of legitimate goods being held unnecessarily at the GB border. Maintaining the existing data requirements (the ‘do nothing’ option) was not taken forward, as the previous government reviewed the data requirements and identified opportunities to reduce the administrative burden without undermining the UK’s ability to secure the border.

• There are no non-legislative options to achieve the desired outcomes.

Will the policy be reviewed? There are currently no plans to review this policy.

Evidence Base (for summary sheets)

Problem under consideration and rationale for intervention

1.

S&S declarations are used by HMRC and Border Force to risk-assess goods before they arrive in, or leave, the UK. The measures discussed in this IA relate only to S&S import declarations (also known as Entry Summary, or ENS, declarations). The UK’s approach to S&S is in line with the World Customs Organisation’s (WCO) SAFE Framework of standards, which requires the pre-arrival or pre-departure collection and risking of information for all consignments entering or exiting a territory. The SAFE Framework provides a global approach to the provision of security data, adopted as a result of the 9/11 terrorist attack in the United States.

2.

The previous government took a phased approach to introducing import requirements on imports from the EU, recognising the need to give businesses sufficient time to prepare and ensure supply-chains have time to adapt. To date, ENS declarations have not been required on imports from the EU, and a new border model covering S&S and Sanitary and Phytosanitary imports (SPS) was published in August 2023. The current government has delayed the introduction of ENS requirements on EU imports until 31 January 2025, with early submission encouraged before 31 January 2025 if businesses are ready to do so.

3.

The government has assessed the provisions within the SI and has concluded that one of the proposed facilitations has net direct costs to businesses that fall within Better Regulation reporting requirements. The impact of the dataset reduction is a benefit while the removal of the waiver on imports from the EU is a cost. The net cost on industry is above £5m and therefore we are providing this IA for scrutiny.

Rationale and evidence to justify the level of analysis used in the IA (proportionality approach)

4.

This IA analysis incorporates policy assumptions from engagement with industry and survey data specifically pertaining to the S&S submission process. The methods have been quality assured as well as discussed with colleagues across government. We provide ranges where there is particular uncertainty, reflected in the final figures.

Description of options considered.

Legislative option

5.

Two measures are being introduced:

i.

Ending the temporary waiver for ENS declarations on EU imports into GB. The data from ENS declarations will provide critical intelligence to HMRC and Border Force to support better targeting of illicit goods, and so that fewer consignments of legitimate trade are stopped unnecessarily.

ii.

Reducing the minimum dataset required to complete an ENS declaration for all imports into GB. There will be 20 mandatory fields, which are needed for security and allow government risking and targeting systems to operate effectively. There are also up to 8 conditional fields, which need to be completed in certain circumstances. Businesses will have the option to submit an additional 9 fields if it isbetter for their dataflows or to give HMRC and Border Force additional assurance on the goods, which will also reduce the chance of legitimate goods being held unnecessarily at the GB border. The full 37-data field set is the same as the existing requirements, so businesses can maintain their existing processes (if they already submit ENS declarations) if they choose to do so.

‘Do nothing’ (counterfactual)

6.

For the purposes of this Impact Assessment, the ‘do nothing’ scenario is used to assess the impact of the legislation. If no legislation was introduced, the temporary waiver for ENS declarations on imports from the EU into GB and the existing mandatory data requirements would remain in place.

7.

Maintaining the waiver is not viable as it would not meet the UK’s international obligations and would limit HMRC and Border Force’s ability to risk-assess EU imports as they arrive in GB.

8.

Maintaining the existing data requirements was not taken forward, as the previous government reviewed the data requirements and identified opportunities to reduce the administrative burden without undermining the UK’s ability to secure the border.

Non-legislative options

9.

There are no non-legislative options to achieve the desired outcomes.

Policy objective

10.

There are three objectives for these measures:

i.

Improve HMRC and Border Force’s ability to identify and stop high harm goods being brought into the country.

ii.

Reduce the administrative burden for businesses required to submit ENS declarations and facilitate the flow of legitimate trade.

iii.

Meet the UK’s international commitments to require S&S declarations on all imports into GB and treat all trading partners equally.

11.

Indicators of success will be high compliance rates on ENS declarations requirements, and subsequently better risking and fewer high harm goods entering the country.

Summary and preferred option with description of implementation plan

12.

The measures will be given effect by secondary legislation and the publication of a public notice which will come into force on 31 January 2025. The public notice will set out the new ENS requirements dataset, so businesses are clear on their obligations.

13.

The government has updated gov.uk guidance on ENS declarations to reflect the change in requirements, and has engaged extensively with stakeholders including ports, airports, and hauliers amongst others to prepare them for the new requirements and encourage businesses importing from the EU to submit declarations as soon as they are ready. Stakeholders will continue to be informed of the changes through government communication channels.

Costs and benefits estimation

Summary

14.

This policy is expected to impact on the cost placed on businesses to comply with information requirements stemming from government regulation. This impact assessment therefore only presents costs faced by businesses specifically related to preparing and submitting these declarations.

15.

The removal of the ENS waiver for imports from the EU creates a new cost. The cost for both EU and non-EU imports is reduced by the availability of a reduced dataset. There is also a benefit to businesses from fewer legitimate consignments of goods being stopped for examination through better data provision, which is not quantified in this impact assessment.

16.

Carriers will be required to submit ENS declarations on EU imports for the first time from 31 January 2025, although Home Office and HMRC have encouraged businesses to submit declarations as soon as they are ready to do so. This is estimated to cost £1582.2m over the first 10 years of the policy with discounting adjustments applied to estimated figures to be comparable with other measures presented to any given Parliament.

17.

The above costs are partially offset by the reduction in the size of the dataset required. Importers from non-EU countries will also see reduced data requirements and therefore reduced costs. The benefits of reduced data requirements is estimated to be £121.2m over a 10-year period after discounting.

18.

The costs and benefits outlined above combine to a Net Business Impact of £-1446.2m. The detailed methodology used to estimate these is set out in the ‘Detailed methodology’ section below. There are wider benefits, such as to the country as security benefits and to trade as reduced unnecessary interventions at the border by HMRC or Border Force, which are not included in this calculation.

19.

The estimates presented in this impact assessment are informed by a combination of HMRC internal administrative data sources, commissioned research, online research, informal engagement, and internal analysis. Please refer to Annex A for full details.

20.

Note, the detailed methodology describes the approach to initially calculate costs and benefits for the original expected start date of the policy (31 October 2024), with adjustments at the end to account for the delay of the policy to 31 January 2025.

Costs

Overview

24.

The HMRC approach to calculating the administrative burden on businesses, which is consistent with the internationally recognised approach in the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development’s (OECD) Standard Cost Model, is to multiply the number of declarations by the cost per declaration. Total costs are made up of:

iv.

Internal costs (average time taken for data collection and form processing) and

v.

Other costs (intermediary fees or charges to access the required software to submit declarations).

25.

There are different approaches to submitting ENS declarations and usually multiple parties are involved, including the trader, the carrier moving the goods, and potentially a customs intermediary. HMRC’s estimate of the burden covers all resource needed across the import process, including for traders to collect the necessary information and the cost for carriers to submit the declarations. We do not break down cost by the type of business involved, so the cost per declaration is the total cost shared by all businesses involved in the submission.

26.

The total cost per declaration modelled ranges from £7 to £38 where an additional intermediary is used, and from £4 to £23 where the declarations are completed in-house.

Internal costs

27.

HMRC estimated a base internal cost of £10, based on its estimate that it takes normally efficient businesses approximately 36 minutes to collect and prepare the necessary information for an ENS declaration. This is proxied from HMRC survey data that indicates intermediaries take on average 36 minutes to complete ENS declarations. A wage rate of £16.90 is assumed based on the quoted wage for bookkeepers, payroll managers and wages clerks from 2021(taking the median hourly pay excluding overtime from the Office for National Statistics 2021 Annual Survey of Hours and Earnings, and adding a 30% uplift for overhead costs, in accordance with the Standard Cost Model).

External costs

28.

Industry research indicates that external costs vary depending on the submission method used:

vi.

Intermediary:

For those using an intermediary, 2021 HMRC intermediaries survey data was used, which asks customs intermediaries for the average base price charged to customers to submit ENS declarations. These figures are presented in the following bands: up to £30, more than £30 - £40, more than £40 - £50, more than £50 - £60, more than £60.

HMRC takes the mid-points of each band, except for more than £60, which HMRC assumes to be £75, and weight these to each band’s share of respondents, giving £37.93 as the average fee intermediaries charge to customers.

HMRC has also conducted desk-based research assessing commercially advertised prices for ENS declarations. The average fee across the six intermediaries HMRC sampled was £26.70.

To estimate an average base fee for those using an intermediary, HMRC took the mid-point of the higher £37.93 from the survey and the lower online advertised price of £26.70. This gives an initial submission fee of £32.32 for those using an intermediary.

vii.

Insourced:

HMRC assumes that businesses submitting their own ENS declarations still pay for the software to submit. Firms offering declaration submission software online have prices ranging from around £2 to £21 per declaration, dependent on the number of declarations submitted per month. Averaging pricing information from the three firms gives an initial cost of:

•

£14.45 per declaration for those using insourced methods submitting a low volume of ENS declarations per year.

•

£6.33 submitting a medium volume.

•

£4.25 for those submitting a high volume.

Bulk discounts

29.

Significant discounts are advertised for those who submit medium and high volumes of declarations themselves (insourced), where they pay for software to submit declarations. Averaging the discounts across the three, HMRC estimates a 56% reduction for businesses making a medium volume, 71% making a high volume of declarations. Stakeholder feedback with HMRC, Home Office and industry stakeholders suggests that similar discounts exist for those who use intermediaries.

30.

Therefore, HMRC applied the same discounts to those using an intermediary on the basis that costs are likely to fall for any business submitting declarations for many movements of the same or similar goods, and if they have automated systems.

31.

The same discounts are applied to ‘internal’ costs, as the costs of collecting and preparing information are likely to decrease for each additional ENS declaration required – for example, if a company is importing the same good repeatedly, they will be able to re-use much of the information from a previous ENS declaration for that good and movement.

Anti-Smuggling nets

32.

Some low value consignments (parcels) are treated differently to general freight. HMRC is not currently aware of any parcel operators charging extra for ENS declarations (although the cost may be incorporated into the overall customer price). Moreover, some parcel operators use Anti-Smuggling Nets (ASNs) which share data with HMRC and Border Force so that ENS declarations are not needed. For the internal cost, HMRC believes that the vast majority, if not all, of the data provided to the parcel carrier by the business would be required irrespective of whether an ENS declaration is needed.

33.

Therefore, for the five largest parcel operators that deal with most consumer parcels, the cost of an ENS for parcel movements is assumed to be zero for simplicity and due to the very low anticipated cost and lack of any evidence on the precise cost. There are many other parcel operators that offer an express service, but their movements have been treated the same as general freight with the relevant costs applied and included in this IA.

34.

Calculations in the model that use costs for staff are adjusted for expected wage growth using the most recent forecast available from the Office for Budget Responsibility. The standard hourly rate of £16.90 for bookkeepers as discussed above is thus adjusted to its expected value for the year in question when calculating administrative costs. This means that the expected growth in wages over ten years is included in the model.

35.

Finally, an adjustment is made as EU customs declarations slightly increased between this initial analysis for 2022 and the latest date for which customs declarations volumes are available (2023). This increases the cost by 13%. Declaration volumes are assumed to stay level over the 10-year period of the policy.

Benefits

38.

While the costs of the policy only affect imports from the EU, the reduction in the size of the ENS dataset affects all imports. HMRC has focused on those elements that fall under the administrative burden and which it can quantitatively assess including following OECD standards on the Standard Cost Model as described earlier. This analysis assumes:

i.

Reducing the data fields reduces the admin burden.

ii.

The burden is the same for each field on the declaration.

iii.

Reducing data fields is likely to influence internal costs more than external costs.

iv.

Savings due to dataset reduction which impact internal data gathering costs do not depend on whether declarants are using insourced or outsourced methods.

v.

Savings are influenced by whether information is uniquely collected for S&S, or also for other customs purposes.

39.

We have estimated the total reduction in the required burden on businesses due to the dataset reduction. Some businesses may choose to continue submitting ENS declarations with the old requirements.

Whole system considerations

40.

A proportion of the goods that arrive in the UK will have travelled through other countries, that is, they are not dispatched directly to the UK. Where this is the case, traders may have to provide the information to multiple jurisdictions. Whilst, from a UK perspective, the costs for ENS declarations will be reduced by making changes to the amount of data or the way in which the data is provided; from the trader perspective it is not as clear that it does make an impact. Therefore, HMRC assumes:

i.

Direct movements to GB (where the GB is the first stop) will be in a position to take full advantage of reduced data requirements or other alternative arrangements.

ii.

Indirect movements (goods that arrive at GB via another jurisdiction or good that remain on board when they arrive in GB) may still have to provide S&S information to other authorities, which reduces any potential burden saving. For example, if goods dispatched to the UK from the USA are sent on a ship that stops at Cork (Republic of Ireland), the carrier will be required to make EU S&S declarations in respect of those goods that are bound for the UK. If the data collection cost makes up a high proportion of the overall burden, or if the carrier is charged a single S&S fee that does not vary by route, no longer being required to make a separate GB S&S declaration may save only a minimal amount.

41.

Non-EU S&S declarations indicate that the proportion of direct movements for non-EU trade is 16%. For EU trade it is assumed that 90% of traffic across all modes is direct. This is because it is anticipated to be more likely that GB will be the first stop after the EU due to geographical considerations.

Methodology

42.

HMRC assumes that internal costs (e.g. collecting information and declaration processing) will reduce by the proportion of fields removed, but only where the fields are not still required for other purposes.For example, if a trader is likely to be submitting the same information on a customs declaration, they see no saving from that field’s removal from the ENS declaration.

43.

For freight remaining on board (FROB), no internal saving is expected as the data would still be required for other customs regimes.

44.

HMRC assumes that half of external costs are fixed (i.e., not related to the number of fields). HMRC also assumes that if there is a saving, it is proportional to the saving on internal costs. It is assumed that FROB external savings are in line with empty units’ savings.

47.

The introduction of ENS declarations for EU imports, even combined with the reduction in the data set, is expected to improve data availability, bolster security threat identification, and facilitate the flow of legitimate goods. By improving our ability to detect and intercept illicit flows at the border through increased data availability, an increase in annual Border Force seizure rates is anticipated.

48.

Home Office analysis suggests that relative to 2023-241, a 10% increase in annual seizures could lead to the additional seizure of approximately 2,700kg of Class A drugs, amongst other additional seizures of illicit goods and the potential protection of government revenue. Similarly, if annual seizures were to increase further, a 30% or 50% annual increase in seizures could result in the seizure of 8,100kg and 14,000kg of Class A drugs, amongst other additional seizures and revenue protected.

49.

While it is not possible to determine the likelihood of these scenarios occurring in practice, they provide an indication of the potential scale of impact that could be realised as a result of ENS introduction. In addition to revenue loss protection, there are likely to be associated socioeconomic benefits from increased seizures such as improvements in health outcomes as a result of preventing harmful illicit material (such as class A drugs) from entering the UK. Seizures of offensive weapons and lethal firearms may also prevent or reduce the harm caused by crimes in the UK. While it has not been possible to monetise them, it is worth noting these additional benefits.

Notes on inputs

51.

We do not apply any uplifts for changes in import levels in future years. This is because the volume of ENS declarations for non-EU imports has not increased despite the corresponding increase in the volume of imports. This could be due to importers modifying their behaviour so that they do not need to submit additional ENS declarations.

52.

There is a high degree of uncertainty to several of the inputs and assumptions. Monte Carlo analysis of the model suggests that the factors most likely to influence the total estimated cost are the level of discount for high frequency submitters and whether there are calculable ENS costs for fast parcel operators.

Risks and assumptions

53.

There is limited evidence available on ENS declarations, so modelling assumptions have been made with varying degrees of uncertainty. These have been described throughout the text.

Impact on small and micro businesses

54.

The S&S new model ensures that those businesses already set up to import from non-EU countries, or those submitting voluntarily on EU movements, do not need to make any changes to their existing systems or procedures if they do not wish to. Those businesses who will be required to complete ENS declarations for the first time (for example, those who only import from the EU) can choose to complete only the mandatory and any relevant conditional fields.

55.

Using the ONS’s Interdepartmental Business Register matched at the enterprise level, we estimate that, 54% of ENS declarations from the EU would be for imports by large and medium businesses. We estimate 46% would be for imports by micro and small and sole trader businesses. This means that around half of the costs would be experienced by small and micro businesses (SMBs).

56.

HMRC has engaged extensively with businesses who are expecting and preparing for the end of the waiver.

57.

On the other hand, as SMBs are more represented in trade with the EU, they will also experience relatively higher benefits due to the reduction in dataset size.

58.

ENS declarations are required across all movements for the policy to work effectively. HMG would be unable to meet its objectives if a specific exemption were provided.

Wider impacts

59.

This measure will not directly impact individuals. ENS declarations are a requirement for businesses involved in the commercial movements of goods.

60.

The competition assessment checklist has been completed for this measure as private industry, in the form of customs intermediaries, support businesses to complete ENS declarations. These measures will not limit the number or range of suppliers or impact the abilities or incentives for intermediaries to compete. Similarly, there will be no impact on the choices or information available to consumers. Therefore, no full competition impact assessment is required for this measure.

61.

The environmental impacts are expected to be minimal as we do not foresee the end of the waiver having a significant impact on the volume of trade, and the environmental impact of completing and submitting the declarations themselves is likely to be small as they are digital declarations.

62.

The facilitations will have a positive impact on Border Force intelligence and enforcement capability. The ability to collect S&S data will enhance our intelligence on imported goods and help Border Force to keep UK citizens safe from the most harmful goods brought into the country. It will also minimise disruption at the border by supporting a more intelligence-informed approach to risking and intervention, as well as facilitating the flow of legitimate trade by reducing the number of ‘false positive’ hits.

A summary of the potential trade implications of measure

63.

We do not expect this measure to have a significant impact on trade. S&S declarations are used globally to risk-assess movements being imported and exported to prevent illicit goods entering. ENS declarations and Exit Summary (EXS) declarations (a type of S&S declaration for export) have been required for trade with non-EU countries since 2011. EXS declarations have been required for GB exports into the EU since 1 October 2021. The EU has required both ENS and EXS declarations on trade with GB since 1 January 2021.

64.

The previous government worked closely with industry to develop an approach that balances the security benefits with the additional burden placed on businesses. We are aware that some businesses will be required to submit ENS declarations for the first time. HMG continues to engage with industry so they are ready to start meeting these new requirements.

Monitoring and evaluation

65.

The monitoring and evaluation of the strategy will follow the guidance of the Green Book (HMT, 2023) and the Magenta Book (2022). This will encompass a range of evaluation approaches including process, outcomes, outputs, impacts, learning and accountability.

66.

More specifically, the impact of these arrangements will be monitored through ongoing engagement with affected businesses, and discussion of how these requirements affect them.

67.

We will monitor the number of ENS declarations received, with the expectation that the volume received for these types of movements will increase, reflecting the ENS declarations for imports from the EU coming in after the removal of the waiver.

Annex A: Data sources used.

Customs Handling of Import and Export Freight (CHIEF) and Customs Declaration Service (CDS): Customs declarations data (2021 and 2022)

69.

This is the primary source used to estimate the volume of EU ENS declarations; since customs import declarations have a good correspondence with ENS declarations and have been required on imports from the EU since 2021. We also use this to segment the population and also estimate breakdowns by transport mode, and for secondary analysis such as the timing of declaration submission.

S&S GB: S&S data for movements from non-EU countries (2021 and 2022)

70.

This is used as a baseline for future non-EU S&S volumes. We also use this to estimate EU S&S volumes for freight remaining on board (FROB), as well as for inbound empty units. S&S GB data is also used to estimate breakdowns of consignment type, such as whether it corresponds to a parcel movement.

New Computerised Transit System: Transit declarations. (2021)

71.

These are also used in the volume of EU ENS declarations, since customs declarations will not necessarily exist for these movements.

HMRC Customs Intermediaries Survey: costs information (2021)

72.

This HMRC commissioned survey, carried out by Ipsos Mori, is used to build cost estimates, with data on time to complete declarations and fees charged.

Commercial pricing information (2022)

73.

We use commercially advertised prices online to build into cost estimates and to estimate the level of bulk discount.

HMRC analysis of Staged Customs Controls (2021-22)

74.

Used to adjust for a one-off policy which meant customs declarations were received in 2022 which were for imports made in 2021.

Industry and stakeholder engagement (2022)

75.

Used for pricing of parcels and to support bulk discounts estimate.
