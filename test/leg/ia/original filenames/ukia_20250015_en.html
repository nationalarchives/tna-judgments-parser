1.

Summary of proposal

1.

The government is proposing to increase the maximum chargeable amounts (‘fee maxima’) for certain immigration and nationality functions set out in the Immigration and Nationality (Fees) Order 2016.1 This will in turn support increases to the relevant fees through a subsequent amendment to the Immigration and Nationality (Fees) Regulations 2018, planned for Spring 2025.

2.

Strategic case for proposed regulation

2.

The proposed increases to maxima are necessary to implement planned fee increases in Spring 2025. The strategic case is to significantly increase the income generated through fees. This will be applicable to relevant immigration and nationality applications, to increase the proportion of costs in the migration and borders system that are met by the end user, rather than taxpayer funding. This will support the department in achieving a balanced financial position in 2025/26 and going forward.

3.

SMART objectives for intervention

3.

The objective of this intervention is to increase the income generated through immigration and nationality fees, to support the overall funding of the migration and borders system. In parallel, there is also an objective to ensure as far as possible that the financial benefit to the department of the proposed changes is not outweighed by costs in the wider economy, resulting from an impact on demand for the associated products and services.

4.

Description of proposed intervention options and explanation of the logical change process whereby this achieves SMART objectives

4.

The proposed increases to fee maxima are as follows:

•

Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) from £15 to £16;

•

Allocation of a certificate of sponsorship (CoS) or sponsorship approval under Sponsor a Worker from £300 to £525;

•

Increases to the following nationality-related maximas:

◦

Naturalisation as a British citizen or a British Overseas Territories citizen: £1,500 to £1,605;

◦

Declaration of renunciation of British citizenship or one of the specified categories of British citizenship: £450 to £482;

◦

Amendment of a certificate of registration or naturalisation: £400 to £428;

◦

Review of a decision in connection with immigration or nationality: £450 to £482;

◦

Issuance of a document confirming the Right of Abode in the UK or a status letter or a non-acquisition letter related to nationality: £550 to £589;

◦

Supply of a certified copy of a notice, certificate of nationality, order, declaration, or replacement or amended version of any of the documents specified under the nationality related Acts:2 £400 to £428.

5.

Following the increase in fee maxima, the government intends that certain fees within the relevant chargeable category of application would be set at the new maxima level in subsequent Regulations. Analysis supporting the future increase in fees charged to end users (rather than changes to the fee maxima) will be provided in a separate Impact Assessment (IA) alongside the legislation.

5.

Summary of long-list and alternatives

6.

A range of options have been considered for generating further income to meet costs within the migration and borders system. The specified chargeable functions as outlined above were the only provisions where the existing maxima level was determined to provide insufficient headroom to enable subsequent increases to fees in Regulations.

7.

A range of options were also considered in regard to the level at which the new maximas should be set. This included setting the maxima at a higher level to provide additional headroom in the event that further increases were to be pursued in future years. That option was discounted in favour of setting maximas at a level necessary only to deliver the government’s specific intention in respect of these fees in subsequent Regulations, as noted above.

8.

The ‘Do nothing’ option with alternative reforms was considered and found to be insufficient to meet Home Office funding needs.

9.

The preferred option is expected to be applied to businesses of all sizes, including small and micro businesses. This is in line with the principle that all employers wishing to sponsor foreign workers have the same access to the immigration system and pay the same fee per vacancy filled by migrant labour. A full Small and Micro Business Assessment can be found in the evidence base section of this IA.

Direct and indirect business impacts and Small and Micro Business (SaMBA) and medium-sized business considerations

14.

As there is no change to the actual fee level under the Fees Order legislation, there is no estimated impact on micro, small and medium sized businesses from the policy proposal outlined under Option 1. A future increase in visa fees to the proposed maximum levels outlined under Option 1 is estimated to result in a relatively small change in application volumes.

15.

Internal Home Office data indicates that 92 per cent of businesses which sponsored a migrant for a skilled worker vacancy in the year to March 2024 were non-large – 78 per cent of which were micro or small-sized, and the remaining 14 per cent being medium-sized. If this distribution holds constant in the future, around 78 per cent of sponsors impacted by a theoretical the increase in CoS fees to the proposed maxima could be micro and small businesses.

16.

Large businesses accounted for 44 per cent of the number of skilled workers sponsored in the year ending March 2024, indicating that just under a half of a theoretical increased cost per applicant could be borne by these businesses. If this distribution remains unchanged in the future, 44 per cent of the additional cost per migrant is likely to be incurred by micro and small-sized sponsors. A further 12 per cent of additional costs could be incurred by medium businesses.

17.

Assuming that CoS usage, organisation size and sponsor composition remains broadly constant over time leads to uncertainty. The burden of the increase in CoS fees will depend on the future willingness of businesses to sponsor workers which may be firm-specific or sector-specific.

Main assumptions / sensitivities and economic / analytical risks

Analytical Framework

20.

The appraisal sets out the economic costs and benefits of the proposed changes to the maximum level at which immigration and nationality fees can be set by the Home Office under Option 1. The analysis estimates impacts following a hypothetical increase in visa fees from the current fee level to the proposed maxima.

21.

The changes proposed in this amendment will not directly lead to increases in the actual visa fees charged; instead, the legislation increases the maximum level at which visa fees could be charged in the future. Any actual changes to fees are set through Fees Regulations Amendments and are reviewed and appraised on an ongoing basis.

22.

The analysis produces an estimate of a net present social value (NPSV) under a theoretical scenario where visa fees could increase from the current fee level to the proposed maxima. This is purely to estimate the potential impact of the proposed legislation. This approach deviates from previous IAs as it does not appraise an illustrative impact from an increase between current and proposed maxima as it is intended that the new maxima levels are likely to be reached in upcoming Regulations in Spring 2025.

23.

The analysis uses the Home Office’s central scenarios of: future visa demand volumes; responsiveness of applicants to changes in visa fees (price elasticity of demand); public spending per migrant; and fiscal revenue collected per migrant. A low and a high scenario are generated around the central case using low and high volume and elasticity assumptions.

24.

In line with previous Home Office analysis, and following recommendations made by the Migration Advisory Committee (MAC),3 this IA considers the impact of the options on the welfare of the UK resident population. Besides the effect on government revenue and processing costs due to changes in visa fees, the Net Social Present Value (NSPV) calculation includes the effect of changes in contributions to direct and indirect taxes, the effect on consumption of public services, and on tuition fees paid by international students, where possible.

25.

As the MAC acknowledges, the resident population is not simple to define. In this IA, the resident population is considered to be UK nationals, and migrants at the point of application for naturalisation as British citizens. For the purpose of this IA, applicants for entry clearance, leave to remain (LTR) or indefinite leave to remain (ILR) products are not considered as part of the resident population.

26.

Additional sensitivity analysis outlines further ranges around the central estimate by varying assumptions on application volumes, public spending per migrant and fiscal revenue collected per migrant. It also includes experimental sensitivities on foregone visitor spending attributable to the UK economy and environmental impacts associated with reduced visa demand. A full discussion is included in the Evidence Base.

Main assumptions

27.

Quantified impacts are calculated in 2024/25 price base year.

28.

A discount rate of 3.5 per cent is applied to quantified impacts.

29.

The theoretical impact of this IA covers a five-year appraisal period. As the legislation takes effect from Spring 2025, the appraisal period spans illustratively between April 2025/26 and March 2030/31. Actual visa fees can be amended annually, so it is unreasonable to assume that this would not happen at least once over the specified appraisal period. A shorter appraisal period has been used to avoid increased uncertainty over the longer time horizon.

30.

The baseline volume of applicants for each immigration and nationality product impacted by the legislation is based on Home Office internal estimates of expected applications over the appraisal period. These internal volumes were last updated in December 2024.

31.

Internal Home Office data is used to calculate average grant rates, length of stay, age of applicants, and (where applicable) migrant income and/or level of spending while in the UK.

32.

Visa unit costs and the current levels of the Immigration Health Surcharge and the Immigration Skills Charge are assumed to remain unchanged across the appraisal period.

33.

The analysis approximates individuals’ willingness to pay for visas and/or immigration products and domestic businesses’ willingness to sponsor foreign workers under different fee levels (the price elasticity of demand) to estimates from academic literature developed in a similar context. Further detail can be found in the Home Office’s A review of evidence relating to the elasticity of demand for visas in the UK published in March 20204 and in the evidence base annex of this IA.

34.

Detailed discussion of the assumptions underpinning the analysis in this IA are included in the Evidence Base.

Analytical risks

35.

The analysis quantifies the impact of potential increases in fees using proxies of the price elasticity for visa demand available in the academic literature. The IA uses estimates of elasticity which are the closest approximations available and do not represent the responsiveness of individual migrants to changes in fees. This means results are uncertain, and should be considered indicative.

36.

The fiscal impact related to changes in volumes is particularly uncertain. This IA uses a marginal approach of measuring the impact of migration policy on the UK Exchequer and excludes fiscal spend and revenue components that are unlikely to vary according to the number of individuals moving to the UK. Under the marginal approach, newly arrived migrants are assumed to have little or no impact on spending on services such as pure public goods and debt interest, or on revenue streams such as capital gains tax, inheritance tax and gross operating surplus. They are assumed to have an impact on congestible public goods and taxes paid by businesses such as corporation tax and business rates. These assumptions are uncertain and the true fiscal impact of such a migrant may differ, either positively or negatively.

37.

Some assumptions, for example length of stay and visa grant rates, are based on evidence from recent years. The behaviour of current and prospective visa holders may not mirror those in the past. Varying fiscal, baseline and behaviour assumptions may help account for some of the wider uncertainties from data.

Strategic objective and overview

1.

The over-arching strategic objective of this intervention is to support the continued sustainable funding of the migration and borders system, by ensuring that a greater proportion of the costs of the system are met by end users rather than taxpayer funding. This is reflective of the Home Office position over the last several years, and in turn works to support the government’s wider objectives in respect of immigration and nationality. For reference, £3.3bn was generated from visa and immigration income, certificate and passport fees in 2023/24 (not including income from the Immigration Health Surcharge). The full operating expenditure cost of the migration and borders system was £9.3bn in 2023/24. This includes Migration & Borders Group, Customer Services (Visas and Passports), Border Force and Immigration Enforcement.

2.

Affected groups include individuals applying for relevant immigration and nationality products, as well as organisations sponsoring individuals to undertake roles in the UK. Other affected groups include those in the wider economy who may be impacted by either a rise or fall in migration volumes, as a result of any impact in demand arising from changes to immigration and nationality fees. Proposals were subject to consideration from relevant government departments prior to agreement and subsequent implementation through legislation.

25.

Visa and immigration products prices, although referred to as “fees”, are compulsory and unrequited charges; they have been classified as a tax by the ONS.8 Prices for visa charges are set deliberately at a level that results in global revenue received by the Home Office exceeding the global cost of providing the service. The resulting surplus is used for activities including securing the UK border against Class A drugs and preventing people-smuggling.

26.

As a result, there is an element of redistribution, where a surplus from the original payment (for legally entering the UK) is spent on activities which are unrelated to the administrative costs of providing a given visa. As the Fee Order does not implement any change to the actual fee level and only increases the maximum level of fees the Home Office can charge, it is not subject to HMT’s Managing Public Money framework.9 An actual uplift in visa fees is also not in scope.

Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS)

27.

The Immigration (Health Charge) Order 2015 requires temporary migrants who make an immigration application to come to the UK for more than six months, or who apply to extend their stay in the UK, to make a direct contribution to the NHS via payment of an immigration health surcharge (often referred to as the IHS). The total amount surcharge payers are liable for is dependent on the duration of their visa. The full amount is payable upfront and in line with other fees as part of the visa application, although unsuccessful applicants receive a refund.10

28.

In October 2023, the rate was increased to £1,035 per person per year, with a concessionary rate of £776 for students and their dependants, children under the age of 18, and Youth Mobility Scheme applicants. This rate came into force in February 2024, and is assumed to remain unchanged across the appraisal period.11

Immigration Skills Charge (ISC)

29.

Employers sponsoring migrant workers under Skilled Worker, Global Business Mobility, Health and Care, and Shortage Occupation List visa products are subject to pay the ISC for every employee who is assigned a CoS when applying to work in the UK for six months or more.12 ISC is applicable to overseas hires (out-of-country applicants), visa extensions and visa switches (in-country applicants).

30.

For the first 12 months of the length of employment stated on the CoS, current ISC fees are set at £364 for small or charitable sponsors, and set at £1,000 for medium or large sponsors. Each additional six-month period of time is charged at £182 for small or charitable sponsors, or at £500 for medium or large sponsors. The level of the ISC is assumed to remain unchanged across the appraisal period.

Sponsorship fees

31.

Domestic businesses wishing to sponsor foreign workers are subject to pay a CoS fee, currently levied at £239 per individual migrant on skilled work routes, and £25 on temporary work routes. Sponsorship fees are only applicable to main applicants (that is, the sponsored individual), not their dependants.

Price elasticity of demand

32.

A future increase in visa fees, enabled by the proposal to increase the fee maxima as per Option 1, may deter potential migrants from applying to enter or remain in the UK. In the theoretical example of uplifting the maximum level the Home Office can charge for visas under the routes listed in Option 1, an increase in the fee level will represent a rise in the overall cost of moving to (or remaining in) the UK, or a reduction of the associated benefit of doing so. This IA applies estimates on the responsiveness of demand for visas to the proposed change in visa fee (price elasticity of demand for visa products) to quantify impact higher fees may have on application for each visa product.

33.

There is very limited academic research on the price elasticity of demand for visas. Home Office internal research has not found any evidence of a statistically significant relationship between small changes in visa fees and application volumes for visa products. Absence of evidence does not necessarily imply that application volumes are independent from visa fees.

34.

To avoid the risk of under-estimating the impact of the changes, the analysis approximates the price elasticity of demand for visas to estimates from academic literature developed in similar contexts. Further detail can be found in the Home Office’s (A) review of evidence relating to the elasticity of demand for visas in the UK published in March 2020.13

35.

The elasticity assumptions used to estimate the impact on application volumes from an increase in visa fees across immigration products are detailed in Table 4. The elasticities identified for each visa category are used to produce a range around the NPSV impact of the proposed fee maxima in Option 1. These are discussed below.

Sponsorship

36.

Domestic businesses sponsor migrant workers to come or remain in the UK to fill employment vacancies. The price responsiveness of businesses sponsoring applicants is assumed to approximate wage elasticity of labour demand. This measure typically estimates the willingness of employers to hire workers (in essence, demand for labour) at different wage levels. Higher visa fees levied on businesses (such as the CoS) lead to an increase in the cost of sponsoring applicants. Higher CoS fees are estimated to reduce labour demand, and in turn the number of applications sponsored.

37.

The central NPSV scenario assumes an inelastic reduction of demand for foreign workers as a result of the increased costs faced by domestic businesses at a magnitude of -0.6. This aligns with research by Lichter, Peichl and Siegloch (2013) which estimates a mean labour demand elasticity of -0.57 for the UK and Ireland,14 and with mean estimates from other similar studies. A low scenario assumes no behavioural response, whereas a high scenario assumes an impact double that of the central scenario, of -1.2.

Dependants of sponsored skilled work applicants

38.

Overseas individuals applying to join family members who are in the UK with a valid work visa are assumed to have the same price sensitivity as main applicants under those routes. Estimates for the wage elasticity of labour supply are applied to approximate the price sensitivity of applicants for work-related visas (such as sponsored skilled work). Wage elasticity of labour supply measures the responsiveness of an individual’s willingness to work (in essence, supply labour) to changes in wages. This is applied to expected migrant earnings (over the whole duration of the visa) to estimate any impact on migrant volumes arising from the proposed fee changes. Increases in visa fees are considered as equivalent to a reduction in the overall benefit of working in the UK (representing a pay cut) and are thus estimated to reduce labour supply and, in turn, application volumes.

39.

The central NPSV scenario assumes a relatively small inelastic reduction in the aggregate willingness to supply labour as a result of changes in visa fees, applying an elasticity of -0.3. This is within the range of the most relevant UK study by Blundell, Bozio and Laroque (2011), who estimated an elasticity of -0.3 to -0.44.15 A low scenario assumes a zero response to the change in wage, while a high scenario uses an elasticity twice that of the central scenario, equal to -0.6.

Nationality products

40.

The price sensitivity of nationality applicants is assumed to be similar to that of migrants supplying labour. The majority of applicants under those routes would have been in the UK for longer than five years before becoming eligible to apply for indefinite leave to remain (ILR) or nationality. It is likely that they have either been in work or have wanted to work over the qualifying time period. The rationale for using estimates for the wage elasticity of labour supply is outlined in the dependants section above.

41.

A wage elasticity of -0.3 is applied to the central scenario. This is within the range of the most relevant UK study by Blundell, Bozio and Laroque (2011), who estimated an elasticity of -0.3 to -0.44.16 It is possible that that the true elasticity would be closer to zero, as applicants would have invested time in the UK (five years) before being eligible to apply for settlement or nationality and, by applying for settlement or nationality, demonstrate they would like to remain in the UK indefinitely. The analysis uses an elasticity range of 0 to -0.6 reflecting the available evidence, uncertainty, and range of possible deterrence risks.

ETAs

42.

ETAs are a new immigration product introduced after the Home Office elasticity methodology was derived but similarities to visit visas in terms of what travellers can and cannot do while in the UK renders ETA holders likely to respond to changes in ETA fees in a similar manner to visit visa holders. The analysis uses estimates of price elasticity of demand for airfares available in the academic literature as a proxy for the price elasticity of demand for a visit to the UK. The price elasticity of demand for airfares is the responsiveness of the demand for air travel to changes in the price of air travel.

43.

The central NPSV scenario uses an elasticity estimate of -0.4, based primarily on Department for Transport (DfT) estimates of price elasticity of demand to changes in airfares for foreign leisure and business sectors from ETA cohorts such as members of the Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) and European nations.17 The low scenario uses an estimate of zero; the high scenario uses an estimate of -0.8, double the central case. The change in the price of an ETA has been applied to the typical airfare paid by travellers coming to the UK from ETA-applicable countries.

Monetised impact

Costs

Set-up costs

51.

There are no set-up costs identified to arise in association to Option 1. This is assumed to hold true throughout the appraisal period, at a point at which fees are uplifted to their maxima. Transitional impacts are assumed to be, and to remain, negligible. No capital investment is required to implement future changes.

Ongoing costs

Indirect costs

Loss of visa application revenue to the Home Office from the non-resident population

52.

If fee levels are increased from their current level to the proposed maxima outlined under Option 1, a reduction in visa applications (as a consequence of the assumed behavioural responses of migrants to the increase in visa prices) is assumed to result in lost Home Office revenue. This is quantified by multiplying the estimated reduction in the volume of applications granted per type of visa by the associated current fee maxima.

53.

Changes in revenue collected through CoS and ISC fees are not captured, and neither are changes in revenue generated from nationality or naturalisation applications, as such revenue is a transfer between two parties in the resident population; these impacts are discussed in the transfers section below.

54.

An uplift of fees from the current level to the proposed maximum levels set out in Option 1 could result in loss of Home Office revenue of up to -£19.8 million, with a central estimate of -£8.7 million (PV, 2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Loss of Immigration Health Surcharge revenue

55.

If, over time, visa fees are increased to the proposed maximum, this may result in a reduction in IHS revenue collected by the Home Office from prospective migrants, which is attributed to the Department for Health and Social Care. A future loss of IHS revenue is calculated as the product of the change in sponsored skilled work visas issued, the prevailing IHS level, and internal Home Office data on average length of visas granted per individual route.

56.

In the theoretical example considered, the cost to the Government from the reduction in IHS revenue due to the change in visa volumes is estimated to amount to up to -£18.9 million, with a central estimate of -£7.6 million (PV, 2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Loss of tax revenue to the Exchequer

57.

Any reduction in the number of migrants may result in a loss to the Exchequer in the form of reduced fiscal contributions, due to a reduction in direct and indirect tax payments made by fewer individuals in the UK. The Exchequer loss is calculated as the change in granted volumes as a result of a future change in fee up to the proposed maxima, multiplied by the average fiscal revenue contributions for each visa route.

58.

This is derived using a bottom-up approach to estimate the expected contribution to direct and indirect taxes from migrants based on individual characteristics and data on their earnings and spending patterns. The methodology and assumptions follow the approach set out in previous Fee Orders and Regulations Impact Assessments,18 with the estimated per migrant revenue impacts (central assumption) uprated to 2024/25 prices.

59.

If, over time, visa fees increased to the proposed maximum levels set out in Option 1, the resulting loss to the Exchequer is estimated to be up to -£632.3 million, with a central estimate of -£244.2 million (PV, 2023/24 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Loss of spend revenue to the domestic economy

60.

In addition to contributing to fiscal revenue (through indirect and direct taxes such as VAT), travellers requiring an ETA spend money earned abroad on goods and services provided in the UK. As such, the estimated fall in ETA grants and associated spend could have wider economic impacts. However, it is highly uncertain what goods and services this cohort consume, and how many of those are provided by domestic businesses and could be attributable to the UK economy.

61.

Home Office internal analysis uses the ONS-produced Tourism Satellite Account framework19 and Travel Trends data20 to estimate a Tourism Direct Gross-Value Added for the proportion of spend inbound visitors contribute to the UK economy. This approach is applied to ETA holders. Using this experimental methodology, the Home Office estimate that the proportion of average visitor spend per trip captured by domestic supply chains is approximately 71per cent after accounting for VAT taxation.

62.

The cost to the UK from the foregone spend collected by domestic institutions is calculated by taking the estimated visitor spend and subtracting the estimated contribution to fiscal revenue through indirect taxes. The remaining proportion is multiplied by the assumed number of trips undertaken by an ETA holder.21

63.

The estimated fall in ETA volumes due to the fee increasing to the proposed maxima could result in a loss of revenue to the wider economy of up to -£734.7 million, with a central estimate of -£266.9 million (2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

64.

Total costs are the sum of all direct and indirect costs, not including transfers. Under a theoretical example where fees increase from current levels to the maxima proposed under Option 1, total costs are estimated to amount up to -£1,405.8 million, with a central estimate of -£527.4 million (PV, 2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Set-up benefits

65.

There are no set-up or transitional benefits identified to arise from the proposed increases in visa fees.

Ongoing benefits

Direct benefits

Increase in Home Office revenue

66.

If over time, visa fees are increased to the proposed maximum outlined under Option 1, Home Office revenue is expected to increase, as higher fees are expected to generate an increase in revenue collected from the visa applications from prospective migrants who are not dissuaded by the increase in the price of visas.

67.

This benefit is calculated as the change in visa fee, between the current fee level to the proposed maxima outlined under Option 1, for each immigration route multiplied by the baseline visa volume minus any reduction in volumes as a result of the change in fee. Changes in revenue resulting from higher fees for nationality and naturalisation immigration products are not included as part of this effect; these impacts are discussed in the transfers section.

Indirect benefits

Reduction in Home Office visa processing costs

69.

If over time, visa fees are increased to the proposed maximum, there would be a lower number of applications as a result of increasing the fee levels from their current maxima to the proposed maximum levels. This would lead to a fall in visa processing costs incurred by the Home Office. This impact is quantified by multiplying the published unit cost for each visa product by the potential change in applicants following the behavioural response.

70.

The administrative saving to the Home Office is estimated to be up to £13.5 million, with a central estimate of £6.2 million (PV, 2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Reduction in Home Office CoS processing costs

71.

Home Office incurs a cost of processing CoS applications per eligible worker. As with visa processing costs, this impact is calculated by multiplying the CoS unit costs by the estimated change in applications by individuals under these routes.

72.

The administrative benefit to the Home Office resulting from a hypothetical uplift of visa fee to the proposed maximum under Option 1 could amount to up to £1.5 million, with a central estimate of £0.6 million (PV, 2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Reduction in Home Office Immigration Skills Charge and Immigration Health Surcharge processing costs

73.

A final set of administrative benefits to the Home Office could result from a fall in processing costs for applications on routes in scope of the ISC and/or the IHS. The impact on the fall in ISC processing costs is calculated by multiplying internal Home Office estimates of ISC by the estimated reduction in applicants on eligible routes. The impact on the fall in IHS processing costs is calculated in a similar manner but accounting for the average length of visas issued on each IHS-liable route. The payment of ISC and IHS applications is processed by a third party.

A reduction in ISC and IHS processing costs resulting from an increase in fee levels to the proposed maximum levels proposed in Option 1, could result in benefits of up to £0.5 million, with a central estimate of £0.2 million (PV, 2023/24 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Transfers

79.

Some of the impacts from the policy proposal represent a transfer between domestic parties where a cost incurred on one side is fully absorbed as a benefit received by another. Transfer payments may change distributions of income or wealth of the resident population, but do not give rise to direct economic costs and benefits; thus, such impacts are not counted in the NPSV of the option considered. The transfer impacts which could arise from a theoretical increase in fees from current levels to the new proposed maxima are outlined below.

Additional CoS revenue from higher CoS fee

80.

A potential rise in the CoS fee increases the cost domestic businesses face when sponsoring workers. The additional impact on UK organisations is calculated by multiplying the illustrative change in fee by the projected visa grants under the policy (the baseline minus the behavioural response of organisations and migrants). This additional cost represents a transfer to the Home Office laying in a range between £419.9 million and £698.1 million, with a central estimate of £559.2 million (PV, 2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Change in CoS liability from behavioural responses

81.

Domestic businesses sponsoring workers may incur lower costs from a future decrease in applications across sponsored work routes. This is a transfer between these parties and the government, as an increase in costs for businesses represents an increase in Home Office revenue from CoS collections. This is quantified as the product of the CoS fee and the change in granted visas. The direct benefit to organisations represents a transfer of revenue from the public sector at a magnitude of up to £1.4 million, with a central estimate of £0.5 million (PV 2024/25 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Immigration Skills Charge liability

82.

Domestic businesses sponsoring workers under ISC-liable routes could face a reduction in costs (a benefit) from a future fall in visas, resulting from an illustrative increase in fees up to a new maxima. This is a transfer between domestic business and the government as a reduction in ISC cost to business is a fall in Home Office revenue from the ISC. This is calculated by multiplying the weighted average ISC fee by the estimated reduction in eligible visas granted. The reduction in ISC liability represents a direct transfer of revenue from the public sector to business at scale of up to £17.1 million, with a central estimate of £6.8 million (PV, 2023/24 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

Resident population fee revenue

83.

Migrants who have indefinite leave to remain or settled status in the UK are considered part of the UK resident population, an increase in the cost of nationality visa products such as a nationality certificate or naturalisation procedure, are incurred by two domestic parties. The direct benefit to HMG from higher fee contributions from the UK resident population amounts to up to £105.1 million, with a central estimate of £84.1 million (PV 2023/24 prices) over the five-year appraisal period.

89.

A future increase in visa fees to the proposed maximum levels outlined under Option 1 is estimated to result in a relatively small change in application volumes.

Internal Home Office data indicates that 92 per cent of businesses which sponsored a migrant for a skilled worker vacancy in the year to March 2024 were non-large – around eight in ten of which were micro or small-sized, and the remaining 14 per cent being medium-sized. If this distribution holds constant in the future, around 78 per cent of sponsors impacted by a theoretical the increase in CoS fees to the proposed maxima could be micro and small businesses.

Large businesses accounted for 44 per cent of the number of skilled workers sponsored in the year ending March 2024, indicating that just under a half of a theoretical increased cost per applicant could be borne by these businesses. Sensitivity analysis

93.

The NPSV estimates discussed above are constructed under central estimates public service provision per migrant, and fiscal contribution of migrants and varying combinations of low, central, and high assumptions of application volumes and price sensitivity. This subsection incorporates a set of sensitivity measures around each of those four assumptions against the central scenarios of the remaining three. An additional set of sensitivity analysis is included which varies volume and elasticity assumptions simultaneously against the central NPSV scenario.

Volumes

94.

Sensitivity analysis has been carried out to estimate how the potential NPSV of the policy could change if baseline application volumes were lower and higher than those used in the central case. This is due to the uncertainty around the future volume of applicants who could be affected by such future increases, and that there is no current timeframe for potential increases in the fee level to the proposed fee maxima.

95.

Low and high volume scenarios are generated to account for the uncertainty of the baseline. For the sponsored skilled work route and nationality applications, the approach is consistent with previous Impact Assessments. The low volume scenario is constructed under an assumption of baseline volumes being 25 per cent lower than the central case. In a similar manner, the high volume scenario assumes that baseline volumes could be 25 per cent above the central estimate.

96.

Low and high volume scenarios for ETAs are generated based on variation in the assumed number of trips an ETA holder may undertake during the two-year ETA validity. In the low scenario, the average ETA holder is assumed to travel twice in the two-year period; in the high scenario, only one trip is undertaken per granted ETA.

97.

Low and high volume scenarios are also generated around the assumed proportion of prospective ETA holders who only transit through the UK. In the low scenario, it is assumed that 4 per cent of all ETA-eligible travellers only transit through the UK; the high scenario assumes that this proportion is 7 per cent.23

Price elasticity of demand

100.

The price sensitivity of individual migrants and domestic sponsors is highly uncertain. The following set of sensitivity analysis attempts to disaggregate the potential variation of the NPSV of the policy driven by the change in elasticity assumptions across the main scenarios.

101.

Assuming that hypothetical higher fees have no impact on migrants’ willingness to apply for a visa (and the willingness of domestic institutions to sponsor foreign workers), the NPSV of Option 1 increases by £461.7 million, from £203.5 million to £665.1 million (PV, 2024/25 prices).

102.

Assuming that hypothetical increases in prices play a more significant part in the decision to apply for (or sponsor) a visa, the NPSV of Option 1 falls by £461.7 million, from £203.5 million to -£258.2 million (PV, 2024/25 prices).

Alternative volume and price elasticity of demand scenarios

103.

Due to the inherent uncertainty of both baseline volume estimates and the price sensitivity of individual migrants and domestic sponsors, an additional set of sensitivity analysis has been carried out exploring a set of scenarios where baseline volume and elasticity assumptions both vary.

104.

In a scenario where observed future applications lie towards the lower end of estimates but migrant and sponsor price sensitivity is higher than the central assumption, the NPSV of Option 1 falls by -£373.8 million, from £203.5 million to -£170.3 million (PV, 2024/25 prices).

105.

In a scenario where future application volumes lie towards the higher end of estimates but migrants and sponsors are not price sensitive to the hypothetical change in fees, the NPSV of Option 1 increases by £746.4 million, from £203.5 million to £949.9 million (PV, 2024/25 prices). This scenario represents the highest amount of revenue the public sector may gain from a hypothetical increase in fees to the new proposed maxima.

Public service provision

106.

The level of average cost of public service provision to migrants is uncertain, so sensitivity analysis tests how various estimates of the value of average public service consumption by migrants affect the hypothetical NSPV. The difference between the low and high scenario is the inclusion of pure public goods and welfare costs in the estimate; the central case does not include pure public goods and includes half of the estimated welfare cost, as not all migrants may be eligible to receive welfare payments.

107.

Assuming public spending at the low scenario, the NPSV of Option 1 falls by £28.0 million, from £203.5 million to £175.5 million (PV, 2024/25 prices). This result implies that the Government will save less as a result of migrants being deterred from entering or remaining in the UK by the higher visa fees.

108.

Assuming public spending at the high scenario, the NPSV of Option 1 increases by £16.8 million, from £203.5 million to £220.2 million (PV, 2024/25 prices). This result implies that the Government will save more as a result of migrants being deterred from entering or remaining in the UK by the higher visa fees.

Fiscal revenue

109.

The level of the average fiscal revenue collected from migrants is also uncertain, so sensitivity analysis has been carried out to generate a range around the estimated impact on the Exchequer. All scenarios include estimated contributions of foreign nationals to income tax, national insurance, indirect tax (such as VAT), council tax, and corporation tax. The central scenario incorporates business rates, and the high scenario adds gross operating surplus and other taxes.

110.

Assuming fiscal revenue collection at the low scenario, the NPSV of Option 1 increases by £5.4 million, from £203.5 million to £208.8 million (PV, 2024/25 prices). This result implies that the Government will lose less tax revenue as a result of prospective migrants being deterred from entering or remaining in the UK by the higher visa fees.

111.

Assuming fiscal revenue collection at the high scenario, the NPSV of Option 1 falls by £36.4 million, from £203.5 million to £167.2 million (PV, 2024/25 prices). This result implies that the Government will forego more tax revenue as a result of prospective migrants being deterred from entering or remaining in the UK by the higher visa fees.

Business environment

114.

As increases to visa fee maxima do not represent actual changes to the price individuals and sponsors pay when interacting with the immigration system, Option 1 is unlikely to lead to changes to the domestic business environment.

I.

Trade implications

115.

There are several channels through which immigration may affect trade and, in general, the external literature finds a positive relationship between the stock of immigrants and trade. At a macro-level high immigration to the UK increases the UK population and consequently aggregate demand and the demand for imports. UK exports may also increase if immigration can enhance the international competitiveness of the UK.

116.

Gould (1994) argues that immigrants have individual-specific knowledge of home-country markets which could enhance trading opportunities.24 For example, immigrants may have a greater a knowledge of foreign languages which helps improve communication in trading relationships, and immigrants may have a greater understanding of legal arrangements which may help lower the fixed costs of trade.

117.

Other mechanisms through which immigrants may affect trade include a preference for home-country goods, which could increase the demand for UK imports through an increase in consumption.

118.

Under Option 1, as there are no changes to visa fees, there are no direct impacts to trade and investment. The Fee Order enables future increase in visa fees above their current maximum level, which may lead to an indirect impact on trade and investment from a potential lower number of business visitors to the UK.

119.

Research suggests that visiting a country for business reasons, be it using a visit visa or an ETA, has a positive impact on trade and investment.25 Internal Home Office analysis of 2018 International Passenger Survey data suggests that over 90 per cent of business visits to the UK in 2018 came from non-visa nationals (i.e. travellers requiring an ETA upon full rollout of the scheme).

120.

Business visitors resident in EEA countries make most visits to the UK, with only the US in the top 10 countries of residence for number of business visitors.26 In addition, compared to ordinary visitors, business visitors may be expected to be less sensitive to changes in visa fees as suggested by the evidence used in the IA on the airfare elasticity of demand.27

121.

Access to international talent continues to be very important for businesses based in and setting up in the UK and there could be implications associated with the decreased individuals’ willingness to work in the UK under the proposed visa fees. Higher costs for workers from other countries to arrive in the UK may create a perception of slightly reduced UK competitiveness for foreign investors looking to set up or invest into a UK-based company.

J.

Environment: Natural capital impact and decarbonisation

122.

The estimated decrease in visas granted following a theoretical rise in fees from the current to the proposed maxima, albeit proportionally small when compared to the wider immigration system, could be interpreted as a reduction in the demand for travel to the UK, resulting in fewer carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) emissions. For simplicity, this sensitivity only considers changes in visas from applicants wishing to come to the UK (out-of-country applicants) and assumes those are equivalent to an equal number of fewer undertaken inbound journeys.

123.

This impact is estimated by considering the weighted average distance between visa applicants’ home countries (deducted by their nationality) and the UK. Assuming that all journeys to the UK are completed by air travel, the weighted average distance per immigration category is multiplied by estimates of the average tonnes of CO2e emitted per kilometre travelled per passenger on an international flight.28 The resulting decrease in CO2e emissions is monetised using the low, central, and high carbon prices set by the Department for Energy Security and Net Zero.29

124.

The estimated future reduction in emissions could result in a net benefit of between £18.2 million and £54.7 million (PV, 2024/2025 prices) over the appraisal period. Under the central carbon price, the estimated fall in CO2e emissions is valued at £36.5 million (PV, 2024/2025 prices).

K.

Other wider impacts

125.

Migrants play an important role in the economy. The impact of proposals that affect the number of migrants coming to, or leaving, the UK will be dependent on which migrants are in scope; their characteristics such as their age, income, health and wealth; and the nature of any proposal (for example, who may come to the UK and what they do whilst here). These factors combine to determine the size of the impact on the UK economy. The analysis assesses these impacts on the resident population and UK economy under the following broad categories:

•

Macroeconomic impacts (for example, economic output, economic output per head, and the impact on the Exchequer);

•

Labour market outcomes (for example, the ability of firms to hire migrant workers);

•

Spill-over impacts on resident population (for example, cultural exchange or congestion/inflation impacts in local areas);

•

Policy design impacts on users of the system (individuals, businesses and the Government).

126.

Some of these categories are inter-related, such as the link between labour market outcomes and macroeconomic impacts, while some are harder to quantify than others, such as the spill-over impacts of “cultural exchange”. Of these, only the impact on users of the system is quantified in the main body of this IA.

127.

While not negligible, the expected reduction in visas granted as a result of these changes is small compared to the total number of visas granted. The macroeconomic effects as well as labour market and spill-over impacts on resident population are likely to be small. Additionally, the MAC acknowledges that the wider dynamic effects and congestion impacts are not possible to quantify, so this IA does not attempt to measure them, but it is assumed they would be small due to the small numbers involved.

128.

Economic output is a function of labour used and capital employed and can be measured impartially by GDP. Each worker is a unit of labour and contributes to the creation of economic output. If all else is equal, higher work immigration means more workers in the economy and higher economic output. Equally, a very small decrease in migration volumes caused by the increase in visa fees may have some impact in reducing economic output but this is unlikely to be significant. Whilst aggregate economic output is an important measure, when considering the economic impact of immigration, it is also important to consider GDP per capita / per person.

129.

On this measure, particularly in the short run, impacts will be small on aggregate as increased economic output are shared across a larger population. In line with MAC advice, it is important to note that although migration may affect GDP per head (by a small amount) mainly due to higher pay and employment rates of migrants compared to natives, it is the immigrants, rather than the resident population, who are the main gainers/losers. It is important to focus on the impact migration has on the GDP of residents through dynamic effects on productivity and innovation and this is dependent on the skill level of the migrants.

L.

Risks

130.

All estimates presented are indicative. The analysis serves to provide a sense of scale and estimated impacts should be read in that context; estimated future behaviour and outcomes are particularly uncertain. The main identified risks of the analysis are outlined below.

Volumes

131.

The visa demand volume estimates used as a baseline for the appraisal are subject to significant uncertainty. This has been highlighted throughout the IA and sensitivity analysis has been carried out to produce a wider range of the potential impact of the policy. However, all results, including calculated changes in the volumes of granted visas and NPSV estimates, should be treated with caution.

Behavioural response

132.

Internal Home Office analysis has not found evidence of a significant relationship between small increases in fees and visa demand. Absence of evidence does not necessarily imply there is no relationship and setting the fees at the proposed maximum levels on certain routes under Option 1 may represent a larger rise in the price of these visas than historic changes. The estimates of a potential negative effect on visa demand are presented; however, these estimates may overstate the actual impact.

133.

The analysis quantifies the impact of potential increases in visa fees using proxies of the price elasticity for visa demand available in the academic literature. The IA uses estimates of elasticity which are the closest approximations available and do not represent the responsiveness of individual migrants to changes in fees. Results are uncertain on this basis, and should be considered indicative.

Fiscal impact

134.

The fiscal impact related to changes in volumes is particularly uncertain. This IA uses a marginal approach of measuring the impact of migration policy on the UK Exchequer and excludes fiscal spend and revenue components that are unlikely to vary according to the number of individuals moving to the UK. Under the marginal approach, newly arrived migrants are assumed to have little or no impact on spending on services such as pure public goods and debt interest, or on revenue streams such as capital gains tax, inheritance tax and gross operating surplus. They are assumed to have an impact on congestible public goods and taxes paid by businesses such as corporation tax and business rates. These assumptions are uncertain and the true fiscal impact of such a migrant may differ, either positively or negatively.

Wider assumptions

135.

Some assumptions, for example length of stay and visa grant rates, are based on evidence from recent years. The behaviour of current and prospective visa holders may not mirror those in the past. Varying fiscal, baseline and behaviour assumptions may help account for some of the wider uncertainties from data.

Statutory Equalities Duties

The Secretary of State for the Home Department (Home Secretary)’s public sector equality duty has been considered in the course of developing the changes set out in this amendment to the Immigration and Nationality (Fees) Order 2016. As this instrument only amends the maximum amount that can be charged, and not the fee level itself, the Home Office consider that there will be no impacts on individuals with protected characteristics as a result of this amendment. Any changes to actual fees charged (which would need to be made through a subsequent amendment to the Immigration and Nationality (Fee) Regulations 2018) will be accompanied by a further statement in respect of the Home Secretary’s Public Sector Equality Duty.
